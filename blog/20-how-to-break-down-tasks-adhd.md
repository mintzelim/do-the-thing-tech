## Why Task Breakdown Works

Task breakdown solves the hardest part: figuring out where to start.

Executive dysfunction affects the prefrontal cortex — which runs planning, sequencing, and task initiation. Research in Frontiers in Psychiatry pinpoints dysregulated dopamine signalling in the prefrontal cortex as the core mechanism. When those circuits are underpowered, the brain doesn't produce a plan. It produces a wall.

Micro-steps fix this by taking planning off your plate. When the first action is specific enough — "open the document" instead of "write the report" — your brain doesn't need to generate a plan. The wall disappears.

The cost of leaving it unsolved is real. A WHO-commissioned study found adults with ADHD lose an average of 22 days of workplace productivity annually.

## Case Study 1 — Maya: The Freelancer Who Couldn't Start Client Work

Maya, a freelance brand strategist diagnosed with ADHD at 29, had been dodging this for three days: "Write Q2 brand report for Helix Co."

She'd opened a blank doc, typed "Q2 Brand Report," stared for 11 minutes, and closed it twice. The task wasn't hard — she knew how to do it. "Write brand report" gave her brain nothing to grip.

DoTheThing, "distracted" focus, Balanced breakdown returned 8 steps. Total: 88 minutes. She started Step 1 within two minutes. Completed Steps 1-6 that morning. The deliverable landed with the client 24 hours later.

Key step that unlocked it: "Open blank doc, create three headers: Positioning, Gaps, Pivots." No judgment. No planning. Just a physical action with a clear finish line.
