## Why Task Breakdown Works for ADHD Brains (The Short Version)

Task breakdown works for ADHD brains because it solves the hardest part of any task: figuring out where to start.

Executive dysfunction is what clinicians call the ADHD brain's difficulty with planning, initiating, and following through — not a lack of intelligence or effort, but a specific fault in the brain's management system. [Research published in *Frontiers in Psychiatry* (2024)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2023.1336040/full) pinpoints dysregulated dopamine signalling in the prefrontal cortex as the core mechanism. The prefrontal cortex runs planning, sequencing, and task initiation — everything you need to look at a complex task and know what to do first. When those circuits are underpowered, the brain doesn't produce a plan. It produces a wall.

That's why task paralysis isn't a motivation problem. [According to ADDA](https://add.org/adhd-paralysis/), ADHD paralysis hits when a person is overloaded with information, tasks, or instructions — and the shutdown is typically outside their conscious control. [CHADD identifies procrastination as one of the most commonly impairing symptoms in adults with ADHD](https://chadd.org/adhd-in-the-news/6-ways-to-combat-procrastination-for-adults-with-adhd/), a finding that holds across both hyperfocused and low-focus states. The problem isn't willingness. It's the missing automatic starter that neurotypical brains run on without noticing.

Micro-steps fix this by taking the planning off your plate entirely. When the first action is specific enough — "open the document" instead of "write the report" — your brain doesn't need to generate a plan before it can move. The wall disappears. That's a neurological workaround, not a productivity trick.

The cost of leaving it unsolved is real. A WHO-commissioned study found that [adults with ADHD lose an average of 22 days of workplace productivity annually](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2665789/) compared to their non-ADHD peers — not because they're absent, but because executive dysfunction quietly drains performance across every single working day.

## Case Study 1 — Maya: The Freelancer Who Couldn't Start Client Work

### The task that was stuck

Maya is a freelance brand strategist, diagnosed with ADHD at 29. She runs four clients simultaneously and loses the first two hours of most mornings to everything except billable work — reorganising her desktop, answering non-urgent emails, making a second coffee. On the day she tried DoTheThing, the task she'd been dodging for three days was this:

*"Write Q2 brand report for Helix Co — compare last quarter's positioning to new brief, highlight gaps, recommend 3 strategic pivots."*

She'd opened a blank doc, typed "Q2 Brand Report," stared at it for eleven minutes, and closed it twice.

The task wasn't hard. She knew exactly how to do it. But "write brand report" gave her brain nothing to grip. The instruction was too big to begin.

### What DoTheThing did

Maya pasted the task into DoTheThing, picked "distracted" as her focus level — honest; it was a slow-start morning — and chose Balanced breakdown. This is what came back:

| Step | Action | Estimated time |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Re-read the original Helix Co brief from Q1 | 10 min |
| 2 | Open last quarter's brand report — read summary section only | 8 min |
| 3 | Open a blank doc and create three headers: Positioning, Gaps, Pivots | 3 min |
| 4 | Under "Positioning": write 2–3 bullet points on where Helix Co sat last quarter | 12 min |
| 5 | Under "Gaps": compare Q1 positioning to the new brief — list what's changed | 15 min |
| 6 | Under "Pivots": write one pivot recommendation per gap identified | 18 min |

Total: 88 minutes (distracted-level estimate)

Look at Step 3: "open a blank doc and create three headers." Maya's brain can do that right now. No judgment. No creativity. No planning. Just a physical action with a clear finish line.

### The result

She started Step 1 within two minutes of getting the breakdown. Completed Steps 1–6 that morning. Finished the report the next day after a break. The deliverable she'd avoided for three days landed with the client 24 hours after she first used the app.

Her words: "I didn't need more motivation. I needed someone to tell me what to do first. That's it."
