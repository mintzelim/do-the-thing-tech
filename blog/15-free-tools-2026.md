# Free Tools for ADHD Task Management in 2026: Beyond DoTheThing

## Direct Answer

Beyond DoTheThing, the best free ADHD tools are Todoist (recurring tasks), Forest (focus gamification), Focusmate (body doubling), and Google Calendar (time-blocking)—each solves a different ADHD problem.

## Tool Combinations That Work

- DoTheThing + Todoist: Breakdown + task maintenance
- Forest + Focusmate: Focus timer + body doubling accountability
- Google Calendar + DoTheThing: Time-blocking + task breakdown
- Todoist + Forest: Task management + gamified focus
- Start with 2-3 tools max; add more only after mastering the first


## You've got DoTheThing. Now you need the rest of the system.

[DoTheThing](www.dothething.tech) is built for one thing: taking a task that feels impossible and breaking it into steps your brain can actually start. It does that brilliantly. But once a task is broken down, you still need somewhere to *put* it, a way to stay *focused* while doing it, and people to keep you *accountable* when motivation vanishes.

The good news? Every tool you need for a complete ADHD productivity system is free. This guide covers the best free ADHD task management tools in 2026 — organised by what they do, paired with where they fit in the DoTheThing workflow, and trimmed to only the ones worth your attention.

## TL;DR

- **Free is enough.** A two or three-tool system beats a paid stack of twelve. Keep it simple.
- **Start with DoTheThing for task breakdown**, then add one tool for ongoing management and one for focus or accountability.

## Why Free Tools Are Enough (The Research)

According to a 2024–2025 report from [CHADD](https://chadd.org/about-adhd/general-prevalence-adults/) analysing CDC data, approximately 16.1 million U.S. adults — around 6.2% of the adult population — currently live with a diagnosis of ADHD. Globally, a 2024 meta-analysis published in Frontiers in Psychiatry estimated that 366 million adults show symptomatic ADHD.

That's a lot of people who need workable systems. And the productivity industry has noticed.

The result: a generation of genuinely capable free tools that rival paid alternatives. The challenge for ADHD brains isn't finding tools — it's avoiding the trap of collecting them. Research on knowledge worker behaviour shows that people switch between apps over a thousand times per day. For an ADHD brain, each switch is a potential exit from focus entirely.

**Related:** If you struggle with [task paralysis and executive dysfunction](post:4), understanding the root cause helps you choose the right tools. Many ADHD brains benefit from [breaking down big tasks](post:3) before adding management tools to the workflow.

The system that works is the one you actually use. And simpler systems get used more consistently.

**Pro tip:** Tool overload is a real risk. See our guide on [why simpler ADHD-friendly apps work better](post:17) — the principle applies to your entire productivity stack, not just individual apps.

What ADHD-friendly tools need to do, based on the neuroscience:
- **Reduce friction** at the point of starting a task
- **Provide dopamine rewards** through visible progress or gamification
- **Externalise your working memory** — your brain won't hold tasks reliably, so the tool has to
- **Surface what's next** without requiring you to make decisions about what to do first

Free tools can do all of this. The ones below have been selected specifically because they do.

## Task Management — After DoTheThing Breaks It Down

DoTheThing's job ends when you have a broken-down task list. These tools are where those tasks live *after* that.

### [Todoist](https://todoist.com/) (Free Tier)

Todoist is clean, fast, and uncluttered — exactly what an ADHD brain needs from a task inbox. The free tier gives you up to five active projects, task priorities, due dates, and recurring tasks. There's no visual noise to get lost in.

**Why it works for ADHD:** Quick capture is frictionless. Type a task, hit enter, done. You can add context labels later. It doesn't demand you have everything figured out before you can save something.

**The DoTheThing pairing:** Break down a task in DoTheThing → paste the sub-steps directly into a Todoist project. Now they live somewhere you'll actually see them.

**Watch out for:** It has no built-in focus timer or time blocking. You'll want a separate focus tool alongside it.

### [Microsoft To Do](https://todo.microsoft.com/tasks/) (Free)

If you're already using Outlook or Microsoft 365 at work, Microsoft To Do integrates natively with your email and calendar. Its "My Day" view lets you manually select a short list of tasks to focus on today — a deliberate constraint that helps with prioritisation paralysis.

**Why it works for ADHD:** The "My Day" feature forces you to commit to a small number of tasks each morning. That daily ritual of choosing your three tasks is itself an executive function scaffold.

**Best for:** People embedded in a Microsoft ecosystem, or anyone who benefits from a clear separation between "all my tasks" and "what I'm doing today."

### [Google Tasks](https://tasks.google.com/) (Free)

The most minimal option. Google Tasks integrates directly with Gmail and Google Calendar, so tasks can sit alongside your schedule without requiring a separate app visit.

**Why it works for ADHD:** Zero setup friction. If you're already living in Gmail, your task list is one click away — reducing the chance that "I'll add it to my task app later" becomes "I forgot."

**Best for:** People who need the simplest possible system and won't use a dedicated app consistently.

## Time Management and Focus Timers

Knowing *what* to do is only half the battle. These tools handle the *when* and *how long* — and crucially, the *when to stop*. For ADHD brains, hyperfocus is as real a risk as distraction: a well-set timer doesn't just start a session, it ends one. Structured time-blocking techniques like the [Pomodoro method](https://francescocirillo.com/pages/pomodoro-technique) have been shown to improve task completion and reduce the anxiety of large, open-ended work blocks — particularly for adults who struggle with time blindness.

### [Forest](https://www.forestapp.cc/) (Free Tier)

Forest is a Pomodoro timer with a twist: while you focus, a virtual tree grows on screen. Leave the app to check your phone, and the tree dies. Over time, you grow a forest that visually represents your accumulated focus sessions.

**Why it works for ADHD:** Visual progress and the mild consequence of "killing your tree" tap directly into the dopamine-reward systems that ADHD brains respond to. The gamification isn't gimmicky — it genuinely changes behaviour.

**Free tier limitations:** The free version covers core focus sessions. Planting real trees (via their charity partner) is a paid feature.

### [Be Focused](https://xwavesoft.com/be-focused-pro-for-iphone-ipad-mac-os-x.html) (Free Tier)

A clean Pomodoro timer with task tracking built in. You assign tasks to focus sessions and track how many Pomodoros each task takes over time.

**Why it works for ADHD:** It connects focus time to specific tasks rather than running a generic timer. Seeing "this task has taken 4 sessions" builds time awareness — a direct counter to ADHD-associated time blindness.

**Best for:** Users who want a slightly more structured Pomodoro experience than a plain timer.

### Google Calendar (Free)

Not a focus tool on its own, but an essential scaffolding layer. Time blocking — scheduling specific tasks into specific time slots — is one of the most evidence-backed strategies for adult ADHD management.

**The DoTheThing pairing:** Break a project into sub-tasks in DoTheThing → add those sub-tasks to Todoist → time-block the work sessions in Google Calendar. That three-tool chain covers breakdown, management, and scheduling.

## Organisation and Notes

### [Notion](https://www.notion.so/) (Free Tier)

Notion is the most powerful free organisation tool available — and the most dangerous for ADHD brains. It's infinitely customisable, which means it's infinitely procrastinable.

**Used well, it's exceptional.** The free tier supports unlimited pages, databases, and templates. There are hundreds of ADHD-specific Notion templates built by neurodivergent users available online — dashboards that combine task tracking, habit logging, and weekly reviews in one place.

**Used badly, it becomes a beautiful system you build for three hours and never open again.**

**The rule:** If you're going to use Notion, start with someone else's ADHD template rather than building your own. Notion's own template library and the r/ADHD subreddit are good starting points.

### Google Drive (Free)

Cloud storage that most people already have. Not glamorous, but underrated as an organisation tool. A well-structured Google Drive with a folder for active projects, a folder for reference material, and a simple document for your weekly brain dump covers most organisation needs without the complexity of Notion.

**Best for:** People who want the minimum viable organisation system and don't need databases.

## Accountability and Community

This category matters more than most productivity articles acknowledge. ADHD brains are often externally motivated — accountability to another person can activate focus in ways that internal motivation simply can't.

### [Focusmate](https://focusmate.com/) (Free Tier)

Focusmate is a virtual [body doubling](https://add.org/adhd-tools-for-adults/) platform. You book a 25 or 50-minute focus session, get paired with another user, turn your cameras on, state your intention at the start, and work quietly in parallel until the session ends.

**Why it works for ADHD:** Body doubling — working in the presence of another person — is one of the most well-supported behavioural strategies for ADHD focus. A 2024 review published by Healthline found that body doubling is effective for initiating and completing tasks in individuals with ADHD, with researchers linking the effect to social presence activating the brain's dopamine reward circuitry. Focusmate makes this available remotely, on demand. The free tier offers three sessions per week.

**This one is worth trying before anything else in the accountability category.**

### [Reddit r/ADHD](https://www.reddit.com/r/ADHD/)

Over 1.5 million members. A surprisingly practical community for strategies, tool recommendations, and honest conversation about what works. Not a productivity tool in the traditional sense, but a consistent source of real-world ADHD system advice that no app can replicate.

Use it for research and community, not as a daily habit — the scroll risk is real.

### ADHD Discord Communities

Several large ADHD Discord servers offer live body doubling rooms, accountability check-ins, and strategy channels. The body doubling rooms function similarly to Focusmate but with a community feel. Search "ADHD Discord" to find active servers — the landscape changes frequently, so current Reddit recommendations are more reliable than any static list.

## Habit Building

Habit tracking apps help adults with ADHD build consistency by replacing willpower with visual streaks and dopamine rewards — two mechanisms the ADHD brain can actually use. The tools below work because they make progress visible and make skipping feel like a concrete loss, not just a vague failure.

[Habitica](https://habitica.com/) turns your habit list into an RPG. You create a character, build habits and daily tasks as quests, and gain experience points and items for completing them. Your character loses health when you skip.

**Why it works for ADHD:** Gamification hits the dopamine reward loop that ADHD brains struggle to generate from plain task completion. The novelty effect is real — and so is the eventual drop-off. Most users find Habitica works brilliantly for 2–6 weeks before the game mechanics lose their pull.

**Best for:** Getting a new habit off the ground. Less reliable as a permanent system.

### Streaks-Style Tracking (Free options: Habitica, Notion templates, paper)

A visual streak — a chain of consecutive days you've completed a habit — is one of the simplest and most effective habit tools for ADHD. The psychological cost of "breaking the chain" provides a mild motivational nudge without requiring willpower.

You don't need a paid app for this. A Habitica daily, a Notion habit tracker template, or a physical calendar with X marks all work.

## Distraction Blocking

Distraction blocking tools improve focus for adults with ADHD by removing the decision of whether to check a distracting site — the decision is made once, in advance, when you set the block. That single upstream choice eliminates dozens of micro-decisions and the attention exits that follow them.

### [Freedom](https://freedom.to/) (Free Tier)

Freedom blocks distracting websites and apps across all your devices simultaneously. You schedule a block session, and the sites you've flagged become unreachable for the duration.

**The free tier** offers limited block sessions per month. Enough to test whether distraction blocking helps your workflow before committing to a subscription.

### [Cold Turkey](https://getcoldturkey.com/) (Free Tier)

More aggressive than Freedom. Cold Turkey's free tier blocks websites on desktop and can be set to block access for a fixed period with no override — including no ability to uninstall during an active block.

**Best for:** People whose distraction patterns are severe enough that "I could turn it off" removes the entire value of the tool.

## How to Build Your Stack: The 2–3 Tool Rule

The most common ADHD productivity mistake is collecting tools instead of using them.

More tools means more decisions. More decisions means more friction. More friction means nothing gets done.

Here's the simplest possible framework:

**One tool for breakdown:** DoTheThing (you already have this)

**One tool for management:** Todoist, Microsoft To Do, or Google Tasks — pick one based on your existing ecosystem and stick with it for 30 days before evaluating

**One tool for focus or accountability:** Forest or Be Focused if you work alone. Focusmate if you need external presence to activate focus.

That's it. Start there. Add a habit tracker or distraction blocker only if you've been running the core three tools consistently for at least a month.

The goal is not a beautiful system. The goal is tasks getting done. Fewer tools, used consistently, will always outperform a perfectly designed stack that creates its own cognitive load.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Focusmate really free?
Focusmate's free tier offers three 25 or 50-minute body doubling sessions per week. For many users, that's enough to meaningfully change their focus patterns. A paid plan unlocks unlimited sessions for under $7/month. Start with the free tier — it's genuinely useful before you spend anything.

### Can I use Notion for ADHD without it becoming a procrastination trap?
Yes, with one rule: use an existing ADHD template rather than building your own system from scratch. Notion's template gallery and the r/ADHD community both have well-designed free templates built specifically for ADHD brains. Building a custom system is where the procrastination trap lives.

### What's the difference between Forest and a regular Pomodoro timer?
A regular Pomodoro timer counts down and beeps. Forest adds a visual consequence — your virtual tree dies if you leave the app — and a visual reward — a growing forest that represents your accumulated focus. For ADHD brains, that external visual feedback creates a motivational loop that a plain timer typically doesn't. If you've tried plain Pomodoro timers and found them ineffective, Forest is worth trying specifically because of this difference.

### Do I need to pay for any of these tools to get real value?
No. Every tool in this guide has a free tier that provides genuine, sustained value. Paid plans unlock additional features — more Focusmate sessions, more Forest plants, cross-device sync in some apps — but the core functionality that helps with ADHD is available free. The goal is finding the right system, not spending money.

### What if I've tried all of these and nothing sticks?
That's a common experience with ADHD, and it's worth naming. Tool-switching is often a symptom of ADHD itself — the novelty of a new system provides temporary activation energy, which fades. If this pattern is consistent, the most useful step is usually working with an ADHD coach or therapist rather than finding another app. [CHADD's directory](https://chadd.org/professional-directory/) lists ADHD coaches and clinicians who can help build sustainable systems with accountability built in.

### Is Habitica suitable for adults with ADHD or is it mainly for kids?
Habitica is used widely by adults with ADHD. The RPG framing may look juvenile, but the dopamine mechanics are genuinely effective for adult ADHD brains. The honest caveat: the novelty effect tends to wear off after several weeks. Habitica works best for establishing new habits during a defined period — a month-long challenge, a new routine — rather than as a permanent long-term system.
