Many people with ADHD struggle with perfectionism. This seems contradictory—ADHD is associated with impulsivity and rushing, not perfectionism. But perfectionism is actually common in ADHD.

Why ADHD and Perfectionism Go Together: ADHD brains are all-or-nothing. You either hyperfocus and obsess over details, or you hypofocus and can't engage at all. There's little middle ground.

Perfectionism also serves as a defense mechanism. If you can't start, you can't fail. If you can't finish, you can't be judged. Perfectionism keeps you safe.

The Perfectionism Trap: You set impossibly high standards. You start a task. You realize you can't meet your standards. So you avoid the task. You procrastinate. You never finish.

This is especially true for creative work, writing, or anything subjective. Your brain can't define "done" so it keeps going forever.

How Perfectionism Blocks Completion: You spend hours on details that don't matter. You can't ship work because it's not "perfect." You abandon projects because they're not good enough. You never build a portfolio or body of work.

The Solution: Define "done" explicitly. What does good enough look like? Write it down. Stick to it. Your brain needs a clear finish line.

Use time limits. "I'm working on this for 30 minutes, then I'm done." This forces you to prioritize what matters.

Practice self-compassion. Your work doesn't have to be perfect. It has to be done.

Embrace iteration. Ship version 1.0, then improve it. Done is better than perfect.

Use DoTheThing to break tasks into small, completable chunks. Finishing small tasks builds momentum and confidence.

Key Insight: Perfectionism isn't about quality—it's about fear. Address the fear, and you can finish work.
