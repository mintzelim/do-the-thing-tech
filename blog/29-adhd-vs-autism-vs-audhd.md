# ADHD vs Autism vs AuDHD: Key Differences Explained

You've seen the Venn diagrams and the long lists of overlapping traits. And somewhere in that overlap, you found yourself asking a question that feels surprisingly hard to answer: *Wait — do I have one? The other? Both?*

Reddit threads on r/AuDHDWomen with titles like "What's the difference between normal autism and AuDHD?" pull hundreds of comments from people who relate to both sets of traits and can't separate them.

The confusion is understandable. ADHD and autism share a lot of surface-level traits: executive dysfunction, sensory sensitivity, hyperfocus, social difficulty. But they run on different neurological mechanisms. And when both are present — a combination informally called **AuDHD** — the experience isn't "having both." It's a distinct neurotype. With its own contradictions.

> **AuDHD** is the informal term for co-occurring ADHD and autism. It's not a separate diagnosis in the DSM-5-TR, but research shows approximately 21-28% of people with one condition also meet criteria for the other ([Hollingdale et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6376165/); [Lai et al., 2019](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30289-5/fulltext)). The lived experience of AuDHD is distinct from having either alone — the two neurotypes pull in opposite directions, and you live in the middle.

## TL;DR

- **ADHD and autism can co-occur.** About 21% of children with ADHD also meet criteria for autism ([Hollingdale et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6376165/)); about 28% of autistic children also meet criteria for ADHD ([Lai et al., 2019](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30289-5/fulltext)). The DSM-5 has allowed co-diagnosis since 2013 ([APA, 2022](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm)).
- **AuDHD is the informal term for having both.** It's not a separate clinical diagnosis, but the combined experience has its own patterns and internal contradictions.
- **The core difference:** ADHD is primarily a dopamine-driven difference in attention and impulse regulation. Autism is primarily a difference in social communication, sensory processing, and preference for routine. When both are present, these drives conflict.
- **Many adults with AuDHD are diagnosed later in life** because before 2013, the two conditions couldn't be officially co-diagnosed ([DSM-5-TR, APA 2022](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm)). Sophisticated masking can also hide both conditions well into adulthood.

## What Is ADHD?

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects attention, impulse control, and activity levels. The core mechanism is **dopamine dysregulation** — fewer dopamine receptors and reduced signalling in key regions, especially the prefrontal cortex that handles planning, impulse control, and working memory.

What this means practically: tasks that don't provide immediate interest, novelty, or urgency require significantly more conscious effort to start and sustain.

About 3% of adults worldwide have ADHD ([Ayano et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0165178123003992)). It's one of the most heritable psychiatric conditions, with estimates ranging from 74-88%.

**Core features:**

- Difficulty sustaining attention on tasks that aren't novel or urgent
- Impulsivity in decision-making and behaviour
- Hyperactivity (physical restlessness or mental racing)
- Executive dysfunction affecting planning, organization, and task initiation
- Emotional dysregulation including rejection sensitivity (RSD)
- Variable focus — can hyperfocus intensely on high-interest tasks

## What Is Autism?

Autism is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects how you communicate, process sensory input, and relate to routine and predictability. The mechanism is more complex than ADHD's dopamine model — it involves differences in neural connectivity and information processing that affect how the brain filters and responds to the world.

The autistic brain tends toward higher local connectivity and altered long-range connections. That may explain both the intense detail focus and the difficulty with integrating broader context.

About 1-2% of the global population meets diagnostic criteria for autism ([Lai et al., 2019](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30289-5/fulltext)). Diagnosis rates are rising as awareness of diverse presentations — including in women and adults — improves.

**Core features:**

- Differences in social communication and interaction
- Restricted or repetitive behaviours, interests, or activities
- Sensory processing differences (hyper- or hypo-sensitivity)
- Preference for routine, predictability, and sameness
- Deep, focused interests (often called special interests)
- Difficulty with abstraction, mentalizing, and unspoken social rules

## ADHD vs Autism: Key Differences

Where the confusion happens is in the overlap. Both conditions affect how you process information, interact with others, and regulate your attention. But the *direction* of the effect is often opposite.

| Domain | ADHD | Autism |
|--------|------|--------|
| **Social** | Seeks novelty and engagement; may interrupt or overshare; can be impulsive in social settings | May find social interaction confusing or exhausting; prefers structured interaction; may miss unspoken cues |
| **Attention** | Variable — easily distracted except during high-interest hyperfocus | Sustained — can maintain deep focus on special interests for extended periods |
| **Routine** | Struggles to maintain routines; needs novelty; gets bored with same structure | Needs routine; finds change unsettling or distressing; sameness is regulating |
| **Sensory** | May seek sensory input (fidgeting, movement, stimulation) or be overwhelmed | More often hypersensitive; sensory input can be painful or overwhelming |
| **Focus** | Hyperfocus is intense but inconsistent — flickers between interests | Deep, sustained focus on specific subjects — harder to disengage |
| **Impulse** | High impulsivity; acts before thinking; difficulty inhibiting responses | Generally lower impulsivity; more deliberate processing before action |
| **Organization** | Chaotic systems that work for the user but look messy externally | Systems often rigid, structured, and very organized — may be inflexible |
| **Motivation** | Dopamine-driven — needs interest, novelty, or urgency to act | Routine-driven — needs predictability and clear structure to act |

**The most important distinction:** ADHD brains tend to *seek novelty* while autistic brains tend to *need sameness*. If you crave new experiences but also need strict routines, that tension itself is a signal worth paying attention to.

## What Is AuDHD?

AuDHD isn't a clinical diagnosis. It's the informal term for having ADHD and autism together. And it's more common than most people realize.

A 2019 systematic review by [Hollingdale et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6376165/) found that about 21% of children diagnosed with ADHD also meet diagnostic criteria for autism. From the other direction, a 2019 Lancet Psychiatry review by [Lai et al.](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30289-5/fulltext) found that about 28% of autistic children also meet criteria for ADHD.

Before 2013, under the DSM-IV, these two conditions could **not** be co-diagnosed. The rules said autism excluded ADHD, and ADHD excluded autism. Clinicians had to pick one. The [DSM-5](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm) removed that rule when the research became clear: co-occurrence is the norm, not the exception.

That's why so many adults are discovering they have both. If you were diagnosed with one before 2013, the other was likely never assessed. The science has outgrown the boxes you were put in.

## How AuDHD Presents Differently

This is where the lived experience gets distinct. Having both ADHD and autism isn't additive in a simple way. The two neurotypes interact, often in conflict.

### The Internal War Over Routine

This is the hallmark of AuDHD. The autistic brain needs routine, predictability, and structure to feel safe. The ADHD brain gets bored with routine, craves novelty, and can't maintain any system for more than a few days.

The result is an exhausting loop: you build a system, you abandon it, you feel guilty, you build a better one, you abandon it again. As one r/AuDHDWomen user put it: "Chaos (ADHD) clashes with order (ASD). Organized chaos — old batteries and new batteries neatly separated, next to a basket of unorganized paperwork."

This internal war is a key differentiator. Someone with ADHD alone struggles with routines but doesn't need them emotionally. Someone with autism alone finds routine regulating. Someone with AuDHD experiences both drives at once — and neither one wins.

### Social Push-Pull

ADHD can bring a genuine desire for social novelty and engagement. The dopamine hit of a good conversation, the excitement of meeting new people, the impulsive oversharing that creates instant connection. Autism can make that same social experience confusing, draining, and overwhelming.

People with AuDHD often describe a pattern: they initiate social contact impulsively, engage intensely, then crash into social exhaustion that requires days of isolation to recover from. The desire for connection is real. The cost of connection is also real.

As one Reddit comment on the topic put it: "The ADHD brain may impulsively seek social novelty and engagement, while the autistic brain finds the experience itself confusing and exhausting. You might want to go out, but the 'social math' required is too draining."

### Focus That Flickers

Autistic hyperfocus is deep, sustained, and difficult to disengage from. ADHD hyperfocus is intense but highly variable — it depends on whether the task is providing sufficient dopamine at any given moment.

In AuDHD, this creates a particular kind of frustration. You may have the deep, identity-forming special interests of autism, but the ADHD brain's need for novelty makes it difficult to engage with them consistently. You might hyperfocus on acquiring all the materials for a new project (an autistic trait) but your ADHD executive dysfunction prevents you from starting or finishing it (as covered in our deep dive on [executive dysfunction vs task paralysis](/blog/executive-dysfunction-vs-task-paralysis)).

### Sensory Duality

ADHD can drive sensory *seeking* — the need for stimulation, movement, noise, input. Autism more commonly drives sensory *avoidance* — hypersensitivity to the same input.

In AuDHD, both can be true simultaneously. You might crave loud music while pacing (ADHD seeking stimulation) but also find background chatter from a nearby conversation completely overwhelming (autistic hypersensitivity). The same person can be both under-sensitive and over-sensitive depending on the context, the sensory channel, and their current regulation state.

### Masking at Expert Level

The ADHD brain's pattern recognition and social intuition can compensate for autistic difficulty reading social cues. The autistic brain's attention to detail can compensate for ADHD carelessness.

This combination produces exceptionally effective masking — the ability to appear neurotypical in social and professional settings. The ADHD part reads the room and adapts quickly. The autistic part memorizes the rules and follows them rigidly. Together, they can simulate neurotypical functioning so well that even the person doing the masking doesn't realize the effort they're expending.

The cost, however, is severe. This level of sustained masking is a direct contributor to [ADHD burnout](/blog/adhd-burnout-recovery), which affects an estimated 93% of ADHD adults (based on a [2021 ADDitude survey](https://www.additudemag.com/adhd-burnout-symptoms-causes/)).

## Shared Traits (Where the Confusion Lives)

Some traits are genuinely shared between both conditions. In these areas, the presence of a trait doesn't help distinguish ADHD from autism — which is exactly why the comparison is confusing.

**Executive dysfunction** affects both ADHD and autism, but through different mechanisms. In ADHD, it's driven by dopamine dysregulation and impaired reward anticipation — the brain doesn't activate for tasks that don't provide immediate interest. In autism, executive dysfunction is more often related to difficulty with task switching, adaptability, and processing novel or unpredictable information. For a detailed breakdown of how these play out, see our post on the [neuroscience of task avoidance](/blog/neuroscience-task-avoidance-procrastination).

**Sensory processing differences** are a diagnostic criterion for autism and increasingly recognized as a major feature of ADHD. Both populations experience sensory sensitivities, but the *type* can differ: ADHD sensory issues are often about distractibility and under-stimulation; autistic sensory issues are more about intensity and overwhelm.

**Hyperfocus** exists in both but looks different. ADHD hyperfocus is variable and dependent on interest. Autistic hyperfocus is deep, sustained, and harder to break. ADHD hyperfocus feels like being carried away by a current. Autistic hyperfocus feels like being anchored in place.

**Emotional dysregulation** is a feature of both. In ADHD, it's more often characterized by rapid mood shifts, rejection sensitivity, and impulsive emotional expression. In autism, it may present as difficulty identifying or articulating emotions (alexithymia), sensory-driven overwhelm, or distress from change.

**Rejection sensitivity** is heavily associated with ADHD but also present in many autistic people. The underlying mechanism may differ — ADHD rejection sensitivity is linked to emotional regulation deficits, while autistic rejection sensitivity may stem from a lifetime of social exclusion and negative feedback.

**Sleep disruption** affects up to 78% of ADHD adults and a similar proportion of autistic people ([Hollingdale et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6376165/)). Both groups have higher rates of Delayed Sleep Phase Syndrome, insomnia, and circadian rhythm disorders. Our guide on [ADHD and sleep](/blog/time-blindness-in-adhd) covers this in more detail.

## Why This Matters for Daily Life

The distinction between ADHD, autism, and AuDHD matters for one practical reason: **different neurotypes need different strategies.**

If you have ADHD alone, your most effective task management strategies revolve around dopamine. Novelty, urgency, interest, and external accountability. Tools that create variable reward, like [breaking tasks into micro-steps](/blog/breaking-down-big-tasks), work because they lower the activation threshold for starting.

If you have autism alone, your most effective strategies revolve around structure. Predictable routines, clear expectations, sensory-appropriate environments, and explicit rules. Consistency and predictability are regulating, not boring.

If you have AuDHD, you need both — and the tension between them is your biggest challenge. A rigid routine feels suffocating to the ADHD side. A flexible, novelty-driven system feels unsafe to the autistic side. The solution often involves building **flexible systems with clear boundaries** — structure that has built-in novelty, routines that include variation as a planned feature.

The [DoTheThing app](https://www.dothething.tech) works well for AuDHD because it provides external structure without requiring you to maintain it. You input a task, and the AI generates a numbered micro-step list that breaks the task into manageable pieces. The structure exists externally — you don't have to build or maintain it yourself. The novelty comes from generating different breakdowns for different tasks. The predictability comes from the same interface every time.

This is the practical takeaway: **understand your neurotype so you can build systems that work with it, not against it.**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can you have both ADHD and autism?

Yes. Research shows that approximately 21% of children with ADHD also meet criteria for autism ([Hollingdale et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6376165/)), and about 28% of autistic children also meet criteria for ADHD ([Lai et al., 2019](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30289-5/fulltext)). Since the DSM-5 was updated in 2013 ([APA, 2022](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm)), the two conditions can be officially co-diagnosed. The informal term for having both is AuDHD.

### Is AuDHD the same as having both ADHD and autism separately?

Clinically, yes — AuDHD refers to the co-occurrence of both conditions. But many people with AuDHD describe the experience as distinct from having ADHD or autism alone. The two neurotypes interact in ways that create their own patterns: internal contradictions like craving routine but being unable to stick to it, or intense social desire paired with social exhaustion.

### What are the key differences between ADHD and autism?

ADHD is primarily a disorder of attention and impulse regulation driven by dopamine dysregulation. Autism is primarily a difference in social communication, sensory processing, and preference for routine driven by different neurological wiring. A key distinction: ADHD brains tend to seek novelty while autistic brains tend to crave sameness — though people with AuDHD experience both drives simultaneously.

### How do I know if I have ADHD, autism, or both?

Formal assessment by a professional trained in both conditions is the only reliable way. Many traits overlap (executive dysfunction, sensory sensitivity, hyperfocus), which makes self-diagnosis difficult. If you strongly relate to traits from both conditions, AuDHD is worth exploring with a clinician who understands the dual presentation.

### Is AuDHD harder than having just one?

Research doesn't directly compare severity, but the AuDHD experience often involves internal conflict — the autistic need for routine vs the ADHD inability to maintain it, or the desire for social connection vs sensory overwhelm in social settings. Many people with AuDHD describe it as a constant negotiation between opposing neurological needs.

### Can someone with AuDHD mask effectively?

Yes, often very effectively. The ADHD brain's social intuition and pattern recognition can mask autistic social differences, while autistic attention to detail can mask ADHD carelessness. This is why many people with AuDHD are diagnosed later in life — they've developed sophisticated compensatory systems that hide both conditions from others.

### Why was I diagnosed with both ADHD and autism later in life?

Before the DSM-5 was published in 2013 ([APA, 2022](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm)), the diagnostic criteria explicitly stated that ADHD and autism could not be co-diagnosed. Clinicians were forced to choose one. The rule was removed when research showed co-occurrence is common ([Hollingdale et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6376165/); [Lai et al., 2019](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30289-5/fulltext)). Many adults who were diagnosed with one condition as children actually meet criteria for both.

- **ADHD and autism can (and often do) co-occur.** Approximately 21-28% of people with one condition also meet criteria for the other ([Hollingdale et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6376165/); [Lai et al., 2019](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30289-5/fulltext)). The DSM-5 has allowed co-diagnosis since 2013 ([APA, 2022](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm)).
- **AuDHD isn't just "having both" — it's a distinct lived experience** characterized by internal contradictions: craving routine but unable to maintain it, wanting social contact but finding it exhausting, deep special interests that flicker with ADHD variability.
- **Understanding your neurotype is practical, not just academic.** Different neurotypes need different task management strategies. Tools that provide external structure (like [DoTheThing](https://www.dothething.tech)) help AuDHD brains work with both sides of the contradiction.
